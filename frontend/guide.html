OpenDrop

How to use OpenDrop

OpenDrop is a free, community-owned map of clothing-donation drop-offs across the United States. No account, no app to install, no cost. This is the quick tour: what it is, how to find a spot you can trust, and how to give back to the map.

Why it exists

Donation maps go stale. This one heals itself.

Clothing-donation bins move, fill up, get hauled away, or land on a map fifty yards from where they actually sit. Most maps record a location once and then let it drift quietly out of date.

OpenDrop treats every pin as a living record. The people who actually stand in front of these bins are the ones who keep them accurate, confirming, correcting, and retiring spots as the real world changes. The result is a map you can trust when you have a trunk full of coats and twenty minutes to spare, and a small, quiet way to keep good textiles out of a landfill and in the hands of someone who needs them.

How it works

Real data to start. The community keeps it honest.

OpenDrop does not start from nothing. Every region is seeded from redistributable, first-party sources: OpenStreetMap, The Salvation Army, Planet Aid, USAgain, and Wearable Collections. Those listings are deduplicated with a geographic and fuzzy-name algorithm, so five entries for the same bin collapse into one trustworthy pin.

From there, the community takes over. Every pin carries a confidence score you can read at a glance:

LowProceed with caution
MediumA second look
HighWell-confirmed

Every confirm and deny vote updates that score in real time. Newer contributors carry less weight than long-standing ones, so no single motivated person can quietly rewrite the map, and a spot is only ever retired when the evidence that it is gone clearly outweighs the evidence that it is still there. You can also drag a misplaced pin to where it truly belongs (capped so it can never be walked across town, and optionally corroborated by an on-device location check), rate a spot's safety and bin condition, note how many bins are present, and add a photo. A scheduled re-sync with the original sources catches closures on its own, with a circuit breaker that refuses to mass-delete a whole region if a source hiccups.

How to use it

From “I have clothes to give” to done, in five steps.

  1. Open the map

    Go to opendrop.site in any browser, on any device. Nothing to install, no account to make, no email to hand over.

  2. Find a spot near you

    Search a city or address, or tap “locate me.” Filter by what you need: drop bins, thrift stores, donation centers, mutual-aid closets, even resale and consignment if you would rather sell than give.

  3. Trust, but verify

    A pin’s color is the community’s confidence in it. Open any pin for hours, address, and one-tap directions before you drive out.

  4. Give back to the map

    Confirm a spot is still there, flag one that is gone, drag a misplaced pin to where it belongs, add one that is missing, rate safety and bin condition, or add a photo. Every small act makes the next person’s trip easier.

  5. Drop off your clothes

    By the time you head out, you already know how confident the community is that the spot is still there, that it is in good condition, and that it feels safe. Community photos give you an easy way to spot it when you arrive.

Safe by design

Built by a security person, on purpose.

No accounts

No login, no profile, no password to leak, because there is nothing about you the project wants to hold.

Your location stays yours

GPS is only ever an on-device yes or no check. The coordinates are never stored, correlated, or sold.

Every edit is verified

A lightweight, privacy-respecting challenge and per-visitor cooldowns keep spam and bad actors out, with no tracking required.

Photos are scrubbed

Uploaded images have their embedded location and camera metadata stripped before they are ever stored.

Nothing to hide

The entire codebase is public. You can read exactly how every one of these promises is kept.

Yours to keep

Export the whole open dataset any time, or run your own private copy end to end.

Free, open, transparent

Free to use. Free to fork. Free to keep.

OpenDrop is free forever, and open all the way down. The code is licensed AGPL-3.0, so you can read it, run your own copy, and improve it, and anyone who runs a modified version as a service has to share their changes back. The map data is open under ODbL and you can export the whole thing whenever you like. Community submissions are released into the public domain. It was built this way deliberately, so it can be trusted, audited, and never quietly enclosed by anyone.

  • Code AGPL-3.0
  • Data ODbL-1.0
  • Submissions CC0 / Public domain

Transparent is not a marketing word here, it is a promise. We do not collect anyone's data for marketing, we do not profile you, and we never sell or share what little we touch. The one check we run, Cloudflare Turnstile, is a safe and anonymous test that confirms you are a real person without tracking you.

Transparency

Exactly how we handle your data

What we ask you for
Nothing. No account, no name, no email, ever.
Bot check · Turnstile
An anonymous “are you human” challenge. No tracking cookies, no cross-site profile, no image labeling.
Your IP address
Used only as a salted, one-way hash to rate-limit abuse. Never tied to your identity, never sold.
Location & GPS
An on-device yes or no proximity check. Your coordinates never leave your device and are never stored.
Photos you upload
Location and camera metadata are stripped before anything is saved.
Analytics & marketing
None. No ad trackers, no data brokers, no behavioral profiles.
Your recourse
All of it is enforced in public code you can read, export, and self-host.
