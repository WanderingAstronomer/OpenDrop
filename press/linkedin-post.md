Last spring I drove past a strip mall near my apartment in Kent, Ohio, and noticed a clothing-donation bin sitting out in the parking lot. I thought, huh, I wonder if that's on a map anywhere.

When I got home, I checked. It was on the map, and it was about a hundred and fifty feet off, planted on the roof of the building instead of the lot where it actually stood. I went to correct it, and I couldn't. There was simply no way to. That small, stubborn, unfixable little error is the reason OpenDrop exists.

A little while ago I said I'd been building a handful of things in relative silence, and that each one would get its own post when it was ready. This is me making good on that. I promised I'd deliver, so here's the first delivery, and it's the one closest to my heart.

OpenDrop is a free, community-owned map of clothing-donation drop-offs across the United States: drop bins, thrift stores, mutual-aid closets, church drives. Anyone can find a spot near them, confirm one is still there, fix a pin that's landed in the wrong place, add one that's missing, or flag one that's gone for good. It's the map I wished existed that afternoon, except this one belongs to everyone who uses it.

I built it as civic infrastructure, not a product. There's no account to create, because there is nothing about you I want to collect. There are no ads, because you are not the thing being sold. Your location never leaves your device. When the map needs to confirm you're actually standing where you say you are, your phone answers with a single yes or no, and the coordinates are never stored, correlated, or sold. Every contribution is verified to keep bad actors out, every uploaded photo is scrubbed of its hidden location data, and the whole project is open source under a license built to keep it community-owned and impossible to quietly fence off. I'm a cybersecurity person by training, so none of that was bolted on at the end. It was the ground I built on.

I'm prouder of this than anything I've made in a long time. And I want to be honest about how it came together as fast as it did. I didn't type most of it into being. I talked it into being. I used Vociferous, a local-first dictation tool I built where your voice never leaves your own hardware, to think out loud in full paragraphs, at the speed I actually reason. It multiplied what I could do in a day without ever shipping my voice off to someone else's cloud, and without letting a machine sand my intent down into something generic. Every design call, every stubborn argument with myself over a layout, every "no, move that ten pixels to the left" is still unmistakably mine. It just took a fraction of the time it used to.

To everyone who has stood beside me this past year and a half: you know who you are, and this is what I meant when I said I wanted to give something back. And to the vast, mostly invisible community that made this possible, the open-source maintainers, the open-data stewards, and the charities that keep clothing out of landfills and in the hands of people who need it: OpenDrop is built entirely on your work. Thank you.

It's live, it's free, and it's yours: opendrop.site 🎉

This is only the beginning of the work I'm dedicated to doing.
